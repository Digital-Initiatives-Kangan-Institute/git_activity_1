Overview

Cockroaches (order Blattodea) are insects that have roamed the Earth for more than 300 million years â€” long before the dinosaurs. Around 4,600 species have been described, but only about 30 are associated with human habitats, and just a handful are considered pests.

Despite their reputation, cockroaches play important ecological roles as decomposers, recycling nutrients in forest ecosystems and serving as prey for birds, reptiles, and small mammals.

Anatomy

A cockroach's body is divided into three main parts: head, thorax, and abdomen. Their flattened shape lets them squeeze through remarkably thin cracks â€” some can fit through a gap as narrow as 3 mm.

Key anatomical features include long, sensitive antennae used to detect chemicals and air movements; compound eyes providing wide-angle vision; and six spiny legs adapted for rapid running across almost any surface.

Habitat & Behaviour

Most cockroaches are nocturnal and thrive in warm, humid environments. In nature they inhabit leaf litter, rotting wood, caves, and soil. Indoor pest species are attracted to kitchens and bathrooms where food and moisture are readily available.

Cockroaches communicate through pheromones and are capable of surprisingly complex group decision-making â€” studies show they collectively choose shelter sites in a way that resembles democratic voting. They are also omnivores, capable of surviving on almost any organic matter, including paper, glue, and soap.

Fun Facts

  • A cockroach can live for about a week without its head â€” it dies from dehydration, not blood loss.
  • They can hold their breath for up to 40 minutes and survive submerged in water for half an hour.
  • A cockroach can run at speeds of up to 5 km/h â€” that's roughly 50 body lengths per second.
  • The world's heaviest cockroach is the Macropanesthia rhinoceros (rhinoceros cockroach) from Australia, weighing up to 35 g.
  • Cockroaches were among the survivors of the Hiroshima and Nagasaki atomic bomb blasts in 1945, though their radiation resistance is often overstated.
  • Some species reproduce via parthenogenesis â€” females can produce offspring without mating.
  • Fossil cockroaches have been found dating back to the Carboniferous period, over 300 million years ago.
  • Madagascar hissing cockroaches have been sent to space aboard Russian Foton-M biosatellites to study reproduction in microgravity.
